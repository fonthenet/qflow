# Nigeria — Nigeria Data Protection Regulation (NDPR) + Nigeria Data Protection Act 2023

> LAWYER REVIEW REQUIRED before relying on this summary for customer commitments.

## Overview

Nigeria has a two-layer data protection framework:

1. **Nigeria Data Protection Regulation (NDPR) 2019** — issued by the National Information Technology Development Agency (NITDA) under the NITDA Act, the NDPR was the primary data protection instrument for several years.
2. **Nigeria Data Protection Act 2023 (NDPA)** — enacted on 14 June 2023, signed into law by President Tinubu. The NDPA is a comprehensive statute that largely supersedes the NDPR for private sector processing. The supervisory authority under the NDPA is the **Nigeria Data Protection Commission (NDPC)**, a fully independent statutory body replacing NITDA's data protection role. The NDPA aligns Nigeria with international best practices and establishes a GDPR-influenced framework.

## Key Obligations under the NDPA 2023

**Lawful basis:** Section 24 NDPA lists lawful bases: consent, contract, legal obligation, vital interests, public interest, and legitimate interest — directly mirroring GDPR Article 6. Consent must be freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous (Section 26). Consent for sensitive data must be explicit.

**Data subject rights:** Under Part IV NDPA, data subjects have rights to:
- Information and access (Section 34)
- Rectification (Section 35)
- Erasure ("right to be forgotten") (Section 36)
- Data portability (Section 37)
- Restriction of processing (Section 38)
- Objection (Section 39)
- Rights related to automated decision-making (Section 40)

Controllers must respond within 1 month (extendable to 3 months for complex requests).

**Data localization / cross-border transfers:** Section 43 NDPA restricts transfers of personal data outside Nigeria. Transfers are permitted where: (a) the destination country is deemed adequate by the NDPC; (b) appropriate safeguards (SCCs or BCRs) are in place; (c) derogations apply (consent, contract, vital interests, legal claims). The NDPC has not yet published a formal adequacy list. For EU transfers, argue adequacy by analogy given GDPR alignment; obtain NDPC guidance or SCCs as a conservative measure.

**Data Protection Compliance Organisation (DPCO):** Under the NDPR (still relevant for transition), data controllers processing at scale must engage a NITDA-registered DPCO (formerly "DPA" — Data Protection Compliance Organization) to conduct annual audits and file reports. The NDPA retains a similar concept of licensed data protection practitioners. This is a unique Nigerian requirement — Qflo tenants above the processing threshold must engage a licensed DPCO/DPO.

**Breach notification:** Section 40 NDPA requires notification to the NDPC and (where risk of harm) to data subjects without undue delay and in any event within 72 hours of becoming aware of a breach.

**Sensitive data:** Health, ethnic/racial origin, religious beliefs, political opinions, criminal records, genetic and biometric data are sensitive categories requiring explicit consent.

**Penalties:** Up to 2% of annual gross revenue or NGN 10 million (whichever is greater) for first violations; up to 2.5% or NGN 20 million for subsequent violations.

## Qflo-Specific Actions

- Nigerian tenants above processing threshold must engage a licensed DPCO — advise in onboarding.
- Cross-border transfer: implement SCCs or await NDPC adequacy guidance before sending Nigerian data to non-Nigerian sub-processors without safeguards.
- Privacy notices in English (official language); Pidgin or local languages optional but welcomed.
- Health data for clinic vertical: explicit consent required; sensitive category rules apply.
- Annual data audit obligation for large-scale processors — flag to Nigerian enterprise customers.
